The Louisiana Purchase

On April 30, 1803, U.S. representatives in Paris agreed to
pay $15 million for about 828,000 square miles of land
that stretched from the Mississippi River to the Rocky
Mountains and from the Gulf of Mexico to Canada.
The Louisiana Purchase nearly doubled the size of the
United States.

The Louisiana Territory

Robert Cavelier de La Salle first claimed the Louisiana Territory. He named the land after King Louis XIV, during a canoe expedition down the Mississippi River. France would soon ced the land to Spain 80 years later. In 1800, French leader Napoleon Bonaparte pressured Spain to sign the secret Treaty of San Ildefonso. He would receive the Louisiana Territory and six warships in exchange for placing the Spanish king's son-in-law on the throne of the kingdom of Etruria. When word of the secret agreement was spread, President Jefferson became extremely worried. French-controlled Louisiana would become a point of eternal friction with us, he wrote in April 1802, and would force us to marry ourselves to the British fleet and nation.

War Over Louisiana?

Under a 1795 treaty with Spain, U.S. merchants and farmers could send their goods down the Mississippi River and store them in New Orleans without paying export duties. For many Americans, this right of deposit was important enough that talk of war began proliferating when it was revoked in October 1802. Alexander Hamilton wrote that the United States should seize at once on the Floridas and New Orleans and then negotiate. Meanwhile, the governor of the Mississippi Territory claimed that 600 militiamen would be enough to grab hold of New Orleans. Also, Federalist Senator James Ross of Pennsylvania advocated taking possession of the city with 50,000 men. Even Jefferson's own party, the Democratic-Republicans, supported a resolution that would keep 80,000 men ready to march at a moment's notice. This bravado arose largely because Napoleon's powerful army had yet to arrive in Louisiana.
